Einstein-Podolsky-Rosen Paradox

Original Paper Summary Recommended Pre-Reading Recommended Post-Reading
Can Quantum Mechanical Description of Reality be Considered Complete?

A. Einstein, B. Podolsky, N. Rosen

May 15, 1935 in Physical Review, Volume 47

In this paper, Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen are questioning the accepted Copenhage Interpretation of quantum mechanics. Specifically, they are interested in two incompatible observables. Either quantum mechanics is a complete description of reality, or, as they hypothesize, quantum mechanics is not complete. They conduct a thought experiment where two particles interact, and then depart from one another. After they have finished interacting, a scientist measures either momentum or position. According to quantum mechanics, the measurement of one particle could affect the wavefunction of another particle. The authors conclude that the only possible explanation of this is that in order for two physical quantities to be considered simultaneous elements of physical reality, they must also be measured simultaneously. The authors disregard this as an absurd idea, one which Einstein referred to as "spooky action at a distance." Their conclusion: quantum mechanics cannot be a complete description of reality.

Section I

Completeness

To assess a theory, there are two questions to ask:

 For a theory to be complete, every element in physical reality must have a counterpart in the theory.

 Does the theory agree with physical observation?

In this paper, we are interested in the first question: is the theory of quantum mechanics complete?

Motivating Example

To answer this question, we will look at a particle with one degree of freedom.

 completely determined by the wavefunction

 function of the variables chosen to describe the particle's behaviour

 any physical quantity that can be measured (observed); each observable has an operator A

 a map from a set of physical states to another set of physical states

If $\psi$ is an eigenfunction of operator A, then:

\begin{equation} \\ \psi' := A \psi = a \psi \tag{1} \end{equation}
where $a \in \mathbb{R}$. This means that the physical quantity represented by the operator A has the certain value $a$ whenever the wavefunction is in the state $\psi$. For example, let:
\begin{equation} \\ \psi(x) = e^{(2 \pi i/h)p_0 x} \tag{2} \end{equation}
Then if $\hat{p}$ is the momentum operator:
\begin{align} \hat{p}\psi &= \frac{-i h}{2 \pi} \frac{\partial \psi}{\partial x} \tag{3} \\ &= p_0 \psi \tag{4} \\ \end{align}

So we have from Equation (1): $A = \hat{p}$ is the operator, $a = p_0$ is the eigenvalue, and $\psi'$ is the eigenfunction. According to quantum theory, there are two cases: either in an eigenstate, or in a superposition of eigenstates.

Definite Physical Quantities

When the wavefunction is in an eigenstate, as in the example above, we say that there is an element of physical reality which corresponds to the physical quantity of the operator. For example, in our case, we would say that the wavefunction $\psi'$ is an eigenfunction, and that when it is in that state, the physical reality corresponding to the momentum operator is $p_0$. In other words, we measure the physical momentum to be exactly $p_0$.

Probabilistic Physical Quantities

When the wavefunction is not in an eigenstate, then Equation (1) does not hold:

\begin{equation} \\ A \psi \neq a \psi \tag{5} \end{equation}

Continuing with our example, for Equation (2), the position operator would be:

\begin{equation} \\ \hat{x} \psi \neq a \psi \tag{6} \end{equation}

In this case, the wavefunction is not in an eigenstate. Physically, we mean that we do not have a precise value for the given operator, in this case the position operator $\hat{x}$. Instead, we have a probability of where the particle might be. The probability that the particle is located between $a$ and $b$ in the x-direction is:

\begin{align} P(x \in [a, b]) &= \int_a^b \psi(x)\psi(x)^*\;\mathrm{d}x \\ &= \int_a^b \;\mathrm{d}x \\ &= (b - a) \tag{7} \end{align}
Incompatible Observables

In the example above, the wavefunction was in a momentum eigenstate. This means that we could determine a definite physical quantity for the momentum, i.e. $p_0$. At the same time, the wavefunction was not in a position eigenstate; we could not give a definite value for position, but rather a probability of where it likely is. This is not a coincidence. We say that momentum and position are incompatible observables, which means that you can never have a definite value for both at the same time.

Position and momentum are not the only incompatible observables, but we will restrict our discussion to them for the rest of this paper. In general, incompatible operators do not commute, i.e. $AB \neq BA$. I won't go into the math here, but if you want to read more, you can go to Griffiths Quantum Mechanics (2nd Edition), Section 3.4 .

Completeness

For two incompatible observables, definite knowledge of one precludes definite knowledge of the other. Any attempt to measure one observable will alter the system, thus "resetting" the other observable.

So, we have two options:

QM is Incomplete EPR Hypothesis

The quantum mechanical description of reality given by wave functions is not complete.

QM is Complete Copenhagen Interpretation

When the operators corresponding to two physical quantities do not commute, the two quantities cannot have simultaneous reality.

If two operators corresponding to two physical quantities had simultaneous reality, then they would both have definite physical values for a state at the same time. But we do not observe this experimentally.
