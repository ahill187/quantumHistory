Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

Original Paper Summary Recommended Pre-Reading Recommended Post-Reading
On the Scattering of the $\alpha$-Particles by Matter

H. Geiger & E. Rutherford

1908 in Proceedings of the Royal Society of London, Volume 81

In this paper, Geiger, who was working with Ernest Rutherford, describes the famous "gold foil" experiment. Alpha particles, which are radioactive positively charged particles, were emitted from $RaBr_2$. These particles then passed through a $0.9mm$ slit, before hitting a photographic screen which detected where the particles landed. The control was in vacuum, with nothing covering the slit. For the control, a sharp peak of ~ 2 mm width was detected on the screen. To test scattering in different material, they tried passing the alpha particles through air, gold foil, and aluminum foil. When passed through all three substances, the distribution on the screen became more spread out, indicating that scattering occurred. The effect was most noticeable when two gold leaves were placed in front of the slit. This experiment was important because the scattering of the alpha particles indicated that there must some dense collection of positive charge within an atom.

What's in an Atom?

Prior to Rutherford and Geiger's experiment, the atom was thought of as a "raisin-bun". People knew that there were negatively charged electrons, somewhere around the outside, and that there was a positive charge to balance the negative charge, but that was about it. In a paper in 1904 (On the Structure of Atoms), JJ Thompson described the atom as "a ring of n negatively electrified particles placed inside a uniformly electrified sphere."

  It was known that there were negative particles within the atom.

  It was known that there was a positive charge in the atom, but it wasn't known that this too was from particles.

Question and Hypothesis

Question
Do $\alpha$-particles scatter when they pass through matter?

Rutherford and Geiger knew that negative $\beta$-particles scattered when passing through matter; but what about positive $\alpha$-particles? And if so, what does that say about the structure of the atom?

Case A
$\alpha$-particles scatter

If $\alpha$-particles were to scatter when passing through matter, this would mean that they would have to come into contact with a dense, positively charged "corpuscule." At the time, J.J. Thomson's model of the atom predicted that the positive charge was not condensed to one place, but rather spread out over the entire atom. Scattering would refute this theory.

Case B
$\alpha$-particles do not scatter

This would support J.J. Thomson's hypothesis about the positive charges in an atom not being condensed into one region.

Materials and Methods

The premise of this experiment was to shoot a beam of positively-charged particles through different materials (solid and gas), and see if the beam scattered at all.



1. $\alpha$-Particles Source: Radium Bromide

Firstly, they needed a source of $\alpha$-particles.

  Certain large atoms spontaneously produce $\alpha$-particles in $\alpha$-decay. They consist of 2 protons + 2 neutrons; a Helium nucleus. Of course, at the time, Rutherford and Geiger didn't know they were a Helium nucleus.

  Radium is element $88$, and was discovered in $1898$ by Marie and Pierre Curie. It was known at the time to produce $\alpha$-particles. So, for this experiment, Rutherford and Geiger decided to use radium bromide ($RaBr_2$), the salt form, as a source for $\alpha$-particles. In air, $RaBr_2$ glows green, due to the radiation interacting with nitrogen in the air.

2. Matrials to Pass Beam Through

Once they had a source of $\alpha$-particles, they needed to select which materials to have the beam pass through. They chose:

  They needed to have a control material for the experiment, one where the $\alpha$-particles wouldn't pass through any atoms, i.e. vacuum.

  To investigate gas material, they selected air, which is comprised of ~80% nitrogen and ~20% oxygen (along with other trace gases).

  A thin sheet (or leaf, as they call it) of aluminum was selected for solid material. In the end, gold performed better, so they didn't include aluminum in the final graphs.

  The namesake of this experiment, gold foil! Both one and two thin sheets of gold were used.
