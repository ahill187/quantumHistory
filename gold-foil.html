Rutherford's Gold Foil Experiment

On the Scattering of the $\alpha$-Particles by Matter

H. Geiger & E. Rutherford

1908 in Physical Review, Volume 47

In this paper, Geiger, who was working with Ernest Rutherford, describes the famous "gold foil" experiment. Alpha particles, which are radioactive positively charged particles, were emitted from $RaBr_2$. These particles then passed through a $0.9mm$ slit, before hitting a photographic screen which detected where the particles landed. The control was in vacuum, with nothing covering the slit. For the control, a sharp peak of ~ 2 mm width was detected on the screen. To test scattering in different material, they tried passing the alpha particles through air, gold foil, and aluminum foil. When passed through all three substances, the distribution on the screen became more spread out, indicating that scattering occurred. The effect was most noticeable when two gold leaves were placed in front of the slit. This experiment was important because the scattering of the alpha particles indicated that there must some dense collection of positive charge within an atom.

What's in an Atom?

Prior to Rutherford and Geiger's experiment, the atom was thought of as a "raisin-bun". People knew that there were negatively charged electrons, somewhere around the outside, and that there was a positive charge to balance the negative charge, but that was about it. In a paper in 1904 (On the Structure of Atoms), JJ Thompson described the atom as "a ring of n negatively electrified particles placed inside a uniformly electrified sphere."

  It was known that there were negative particles within the atom.

  It was known that there was a positive charge in the atom, but it wasn't known that this too was from particles.
